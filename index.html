Origin

Where I'm From Shaped Everything I'm Building Toward

I grew up in Elizabeth, West Virginia — Wirt County. A county of fewer than 4,000 people where the economy had long moved on, but the people hadn't forgotten how to take care of each other. That's not nostalgia. That's a model.

I was a first-generation college student caught between two worlds. One side of my family had never entered the professional workforce. The other worked themselves to the bone and didn't always have the time to be present. I had no blueprint. What I had were people who showed up anyway.

A music teacher named Robert Lowe who handed me a restored 1930s saxophone he found in a Cleveland attic — not because he had to, but because he saw something worth investing in. A third-grade teacher who opened her home during my senior year so I could make marching band practice. People who had no obligation to me, but showed up like neighbors.

Those people changed the trajectory of my life. And I've spent every year since trying to figure out how to build more of that — in every team I join, every person I train, and every community I'm part of.

That's not a soft value. That's a strategy. The most connected teams move faster. The most trusting organizations retain better. The most neighborly communities produce more. I'm here to build all three.

May 9, 1863 — Burning Springs, WV

The Place That Fueled a Nation and Got Left Behind

Burning Springs was once one of only two oil-producing fields in the entire world. A boomtown of 10,000 people grew overnight. Its wealth may have funded West Virginia's statehood. Confederate General William Jones burned it to the ground in what became the first military oil strike in the history of warfare.

The wealth left. The people stayed. The county never fully recovered.

What I Believe

The Convictions That
Guide How I Work

These aren't values from a company handbook. They're conclusions I've drawn from where I've been — and the direction I'm heading.

01

The most underutilized resource in any organization is people who already know the answer but don't feel safe saying it.

Psychological safety isn't soft. It's a performance variable. My job as a leader is to create the conditions for it.

02

Fragmentation is expensive. The cost of people not knowing each other shows up everywhere — in turnover, in misalignment, in missed ideas.

Every team I join gets more connected than when I arrived. That's not an accident. It's intentional.

03

You don't need the perfect background to do excellent work. You need the clarity to know what you're building and why.

I came from Wirt County. I'll outwork, outlearn, and out-care almost anyone in the room. And I'll help the next person do the same.

04

Data tells you what happened. Curiosity tells you why. The best operators need both.

I'm building the analytical toolkit to back the instincts I've spent a lifetime sharpening.

05

Showing up is a strategy. Most people underestimate how much ground you cover just by being genuinely present.

A teacher opened her home so I could make band practice. A mentor handed me a saxophone. Presence changes lives.

06

The world doesn't need more people optimizing for themselves. It needs more people building things that last longer than their ambition.

My career is Chapter 1. The rest of the story is about what I can give back to places like where I came from.
