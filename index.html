Origin

Where I'm From Shaped Everything I'm Building Toward

I grew up in Elizabeth, West Virginia — Wirt County. Fewer than 4,000 people. An economy the modern world had long moved past. But a community that still remembered how to take care of each other. That's not nostalgia. That's a model.

I was a first-generation college student caught between two worlds — one side of my family that had never entered the professional workforce, and another that worked themselves to the bone and didn't always have time to be present. I had no blueprint. What I had were people who showed up anyway.

A music teacher named Robert Lowe handed me a restored 1930s saxophone he found in a Cleveland attic — not because he had to, but because he saw something worth investing in. A third-grade teacher opened her home during my senior year so I could make marching band practice. That's the kind of community I came from. And it's the kind of world I'm spending my life trying to rebuild — at every scale I can reach.

I walked into Marshall University as a music education major — Robert Lowe's influence visible on my first transcript. I transferred schools, changed direction, found business, and stumbled my first semester in the Brad D. Smith School of Business. Then I rebuilt. Then I finished Magna Cum Laude.

Every role since has been a different laboratory for the same experiment: what happens when people are more connected, more trusted, and more invested in each other's success? The answer is always the same. Things get better.

May 9, 1863 — Burning Springs, WV

The Place That Fueled a Nation and Got Left Behind

Burning Springs was once one of only two oil-producing fields in the entire world. A boomtown of 10,000 people grew overnight. Its wealth may have helped fund West Virginia's statehood. Confederate General William Jones burned it to the ground — the first military oil strike in the history of warfare.

The wealth left. The people stayed. The county never fully recovered. That's the divide I'm spending my life trying to close.

What I Believe

The Convictions That
Guide How I Work

These aren't values from a company handbook. They're conclusions drawn from where I've been — and the direction I'm heading.

01

The most underutilized resource in any organization is people who already know the answer but don't feel safe saying it.

Psychological safety isn't soft. It's a performance variable. My job as a leader is to create the conditions for it.

02

Fragmentation is expensive. The cost of people not knowing each other shows up everywhere — in turnover, misalignment, and missed ideas.

Every team I join gets more connected than when I arrived. That's not an accident. It's intentional.

03

You don't need the perfect background to do excellent work. You need clarity on what you're building and why.

I came from Wirt County. I'll outwork, outlearn, and out-care almost anyone in the room — and help the next person do the same.

04

Data tells you what happened. Curiosity tells you why. The best operators need both.

I've built analytics infrastructure from scratch and used it to drive results. The numbers matter — but so does the question behind them.

05

Showing up is a strategy. Most people underestimate how much ground you cover by being genuinely present.

A teacher opened her home so I could make band practice. A mentor handed me a saxophone. Presence changes lives.

06

The world doesn't need more people optimizing for themselves. It needs more people building things that outlast their ambition.

My career is Chapter 1. The rest of the story is about what I can give back to places like where I came from.
